Traits are things a character is or becomes through their life in the lobby and the tower. They are distinct from stats, archetype and skills — they define personality, capability and role in ways that bleed into combat behaviour, world activity and the texture of who a character is.

---

## Two Kinds of Trait

Traits cover two overlapping spaces without needing to be formally separated.

**Personality traits** describe how a character behaves: reckless, guarded, stubborn, calm. They feed into auto-battle tendencies, bond formation speed, and how a character responds to situations in the world.

**Vocation traits** describe what a character is good at outside of combat: blacksmith, researcher, leader, trainer, gatherer. They unlock activities in the lobby and on expeditions, and define what role a character plays in the broader world beyond fighting.

A trait is a trait. The distinction is for design reference, not something the player needs to learn.

---

## Trait Slots

Every character has a hidden trait slot cap determined at summon and increasing at star up, aptitude gives a higher chance for them to unlock. Most characters cap at two. Occasionally a character can hold three or four. Rarely one. The cap is never shown directly — players discover it by noticing a character keeps developing or has stopped.

Additional trait slots can be gained at star ascension. Each ascension rolls a chance to open a new slot, weighted by the character's aptitude score. This means a character raised from 1 star through to 6 star has had multiple chances to expand, and a summoned 4 star has had those same rolls happen offscreen arriving with slots already filled and traits already developed, reflecting their more advanced starting state.

---

## How Traits Unlock

Traits are not unlocked by completing a checklist. They emerge from conditions being met repeatedly or meaningfully, combined with a hidden per-character aptitude for that specific trait.

Every trait has a hidden progress value per character. That value moves through relevant experience — the right activities, the right moments, the right company. But each character has a hidden ceiling for each trait. Some characters the leadership bar climbs quickly. Some it never moves at all because the aptitude simply is not there. Some it starts moving then stops before unlock.

A trait unlock is never guaranteed. Conditions make it possible. Aptitude determines how possible.

---

## Aptitude & The Two Growth Rates

Every character has two hidden values that govern how they develop.

**Growth** covers everything mechanical — XP gain rate, how generously stat thresholds reward the character at levels 20, 40 and 60, and how quickly weapon techniques develop through use. A high growth character visibly outpaces a low growth character in raw combat capability given the same investment of time and resources. This is the growth rate referenced elsewhere in the design documents.

**Aptitude** covers trait development specifically — how quickly an individual trait's hidden progress moves, how high each trait's personal ceiling sits for this character, and how likely ascension rolls are to open a new trait slot. A high aptitude character accumulates traits more readily, reaches their ceilings faster, and tends toward a higher final slot count. A low aptitude character develops traits slowly if at all, and may plateau at one or two regardless of how long they are played.

The two are independent. A character can have high growth and low aptitude — a formidable fighter who remains mechanically simple as a person. Or low growth and high aptitude — someone who develops slowly as a combatant but accumulates surprising depth through lived experience. Characters with both high are genuinely rare and feel like a significant find. They are not designed to exist at any particular rarity tier, just uncommon enough that discovering one feels meaningful.

Both growth and aptitude can increase through play. Bonds with other characters, specific missions, and significant moments in a character's development can nudge either value upward. Neither is fixed at summon, just seeded there. Occasionally a player discovers a character has grown into something they did not expect, which is the intended experience.

## Memory Fragments & Trait Seeding

Memory fragments do not directly unlock traits but they seed them. A fragment describing a character as a former academy instructor signals that the trainer vocation is reachable. A fragment showing them as someone who always fought alone signals a guarded or solo-leaning personality. Players who engage with fragments gain a sense of what to develop toward. Players who skip them develop traits more organically through whatever activities they naturally use the character for.

Neither path is wrong. The fragment just gives the player a head start on reading the character.

## Flat and Evolving Traits

Traits are split into two types.

**Flat traits** describe a state of being — reckless, guarded, calm, stubborn. There is no natural axis of progression. A more reckless reckless does not mean anything. These traits do not develop on their own and their mechanical expression does not deepen over time. They are simply part of who the character is.

**Evolving traits** describe a practice or a role — blacksmith, researcher, leader, trainer, gatherer. These have depth and can develop through tiers. A novice blacksmith and a master blacksmith are meaningfully different. Each tier unlocks through relevant experience and then rolls whether a further tier exists for this character. Some blacksmiths cap at intermediate. Others reach master. The ceiling is hidden and set per character, influenced by aptitude. The interesting question is not whether a character progresses but how far they go.

Evolution within a trait is always opt-in when it fires. The player is notified that development is possible and chooses whether to allow it. A trait you built a strategy around will never change without your consent.

---
## Trait Merging

Some traits can merge into something neither alone could produce. Leader and trainer combining into mentor. Blacksmith and researcher combining into artificer. Two leadership variants like leader and visionary combining into commander. The merged result carries the mechanical expression of both parents and represents a direction no single trait could reach.

Merging uses the same underlying roll as evolution. Every trait on acquisition rolls whether it can participate in any of its possible merges — this happens once for flat traits at the moment they are acquired, and again at each development stage for evolving traits. If two traits that have both rolled into the same merge destination are present on the same character, the merge becomes possible. It does not fire immediately. The character needs time and the right situations for both traits to be expressed together before it crystallises.

Because both traits must independently roll toward the same destination, merges are genuinely rare. A character can have both leader and trainer for a long time and never produce mentor, because one or both never rolled that direction. Another character produces the merge quickly because both rolled into it at acquisition. The player never knows which situation they are in until it happens or clearly does not.

Merging frees a slot. Two traits becoming one leaves room for something new to develop, which is its own reward beyond the merged result.

The specific merge trees — which traits can combine into what, and what branches exist within evolving traits — are defined as the trait list is built out. The above is the structural logic that governs all of them.
