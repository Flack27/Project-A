Manhwa

The components that achieve manhwa style in 3D are: a toon shader with sharp shadow ramps (2-3 distinct light bands instead of gradient shading), an outline shader (either inverted hull or post-process — both are off-the-shelf), specific hair shaders with anisotropic highlights for that flat shine, eye shaders that handle the iris and highlight specifically, rim lighting for that edge glow, and post-processing for color grading and bloom. Every one of these exists as ready-made shaders or plugins. Unity has UTS3 (free), the Anime Toon Shader package, several paid options ranging from $20-100. Unreal has equivalents in Fab. You're buying or licensing the shader stack, tuning parameters, not writing rendering code from scratch.

For the AI generation side, manhwa is also one of the friendliest styles to generate consistently. Tons of training data exists, the style markers are distinctive enough that LoRAs and style references can lock generation tightly, and identity preservation across evolution stages (your 1 star → 5 star arc) works well in this aesthetic specifically because the rendering style is so consistent it doesn't drift much. Realistic art generation drifts wildly across passes; stylized manhwa stays much more on-model.

So the answer to your underlying worry — can a non-artist solo dev achieve this look — is much more "yes" than for almost any other aesthetic you could have picked. The work shifts from "drawing skill" to "shader tuning and style discipline," which are dev-friendly skills. Your character art is AI-generated and matches the style by default. Your 3D meshes get cel-shaded with that same style. Your card and your in-game character read as the same character because both are wearing the manhwa "filter" applied via shaders rather than being separate art passes.
