### What is this game?

Most gacha games are about getting the rare character. The rarer the pull, the stronger the unit, the more the game pushes you toward chasing the next banner. This isn't that. The core idea is a turn-based RPG where the point is raising characters, not collecting them. A 1 star you've invested in can genuinely hold their own against a 4 star someone else pulled and never developed. Higher rarity characters start with more going for them, but the ceiling is reachable from anywhere if you put in the work. Every character is unique and randomly summoned, you never know exactly who is going to come through. Different races, backstories, and development potential. Part of the appeal is working with who shows up rather than chasing a specific unit, and the process of figuring out what someone can become is a big part of what makes raising them interesting. **The setting:** the world has been frozen in stasis. Time just stopped, everyone in it suspended mid-moment. The characters you summon were pulled out before the freeze took them. They wake up in the lobby with no memory of how they got there. What caused the stasis, why these specific people got pulled, what's actually at the top none of that is answered early. The lobby is your home base. Characters live there and you upgrade it unlocking more buildings and utilities. The tower rises from the center of it. No visible ceiling. Floors are a real challenge, they require actual team investment built over time. Big systems unlock as you climb rather than being handed to you upfront.

### How characters develop

Characters progress through star tiers, from 1 to 6 with 7 as an endgame ceiling. Each tier has a level cap of 10, so 1 star runs from level 1 to 10, 2 star from 11 to 20, and so on. Hitting the level cap and having the right materials is what triggers an ascension to the next star. The star tier is the main measure of how developed a character is. Weapons are swappable at any time. Whatever weapon a character is using has a technique tree attached to it, a set of learnable active skills that develop through combat use. You can see the full tree upfront so you can plan, and you can retrain or swap weapons whenever you want. The techniques a character builds early matter though, because they influence what class options appear later. Here is how the full progression looks: **1 star** — first weapon skill slot available, techniques start developing through combat. **2 star** — elemental awakening resolves. The character either awakens to an element or they don't. This is influenced by race, elves lean toward wind, dwarves toward earth, etc. but nothing is guaranteed. A fire awakening opens fire mage and fire-aligned support paths alongside the standard options. Light and dark awakenings are rare across all races and open unique class variants with abilities nothing else can access. No awakening means a purely physical path, which is not a downside, just a different direction. Second weapon skill slot opens here. **3 star** — class locks in permanently based on weapon techniques developed and awakening result. First class skill unlocks, first class passive unlocks. A sword user who built defensively sees tank and guardian options. One who went offensive sees warrior and assassin. The class skills are permanent once chosen. The weapon skill slots stay flexible throughout. **4 star** — second class skill unlocks, deepening the kit established at 3 star. Second passive unlocks. **5 star** — third passive unlocks. No new active skills from here, the kit is complete. Investment from this point is about refining what exists. **6 star** — fourth passive unlocks. By 3 star you have a character with a defined identity that reflects how you raised them. Two characters of the same race raised differently can end up in completely different places. The game is designed so you give characters a chance before writing them off, and sometimes that pays off in ways you didn't see coming.

### Combat

The combat will feel familiar if you've played any turn-based idle RPG. You build a party of four characters and fight through enemy encounters, each character acting in turn order determined by their speed stat. Faster characters act more frequently and can lap slower ones entirely, making speed one of the most important things to build around. Each character has four active skills, two from their weapon tree and two locked in by their class, plus a basic attack that's always available. That's the standard stuff. Where it gets more interesting is the auto battle system. **Auto battle** lets your party fight without any input from you, which fits the background-game design goal. But it's not just "use whatever skill is off cooldown." Auto battle intelligence scales with how trained a character is. A freshly summoned character will use whatever skill is off cooldown. A well developed one reads the fight, targets intelligently, uses skills situationally rather than on cooldown. More importantly, characters who train and fight together start playing around each other. A support learns to hold a cleanse for the specific debuff that the enemy team likes to apply rather than burning it immediately. A damage dealer learns to set up after the buffer has acted rather than before. Teams that have fought together develop a chemistry that random groups of well-built characters don't have. This means two players with identical characters can have completely different results depending on how they raised them and who they raised them alongside. You can also jump into manual play at any time for full control, and nothing is locked behind playing manually. Auto battle is a ceiling you raise through investment, not a shortcut that replaces skill.
